How to Plan a Group Tour of 20+ People — Complete Checklist

✍ Avadhut Tours Team 📅 June 2026 📖 6 min read 📍 All India

Group travel is one of the most joyful experiences you can have — shared meals, shared sights, shared laughter, and the unique energy that only comes from experiencing something with people you know. But it's also one of the most logistically demanding forms of travel there is. One person's dietary requirement, one delayed flight, one wrong hotel booking — and suddenly you've got 25 people standing in a car park at midnight with nowhere to go.

We've organised group tours for everything from school reunions and kitty parties to corporate offsites and housing society trips — groups ranging from 15 to 200 people. Here's the complete planning checklist, drawn from real experience.

Section 1 — Start Early: Book 3 to 6 Months in Advance

Why the timeline matters more for groups

For individuals or couples, booking 3–4 weeks ahead is usually fine. For groups of 20+, that timeline is dangerously short. Here's what runs out fast when you wait:

  • Contiguous hotel rooms — hotels can accommodate a group of 20, but getting rooms on the same floor, or even in the same wing, requires early booking. Scattered rooms make coordination a nightmare.
  • Group vehicle availability — a proper AC mini-bus or coach with an experienced mountain driver is not something you can book last minute during peak season. During May–June, vehicles are fully committed 2–3 months ahead.
  • Entry permits — destinations like Ladakh, certain wildlife sanctuaries, and parts of Northeast India require permits that can take time to arrange for large groups.
  • Restaurant reservations — a party of 25 needs pre-booking at most decent restaurants, especially in smaller hill station towns.

Recommended booking lead time: 3 months minimum for domestic group tours; 5–6 months for Kashmir, Ladakh, or Northeast India.

Section 2 — Budget Planning Done Right

How to structure your group budget

Group travel can be significantly cheaper per person than individual travel — if you budget correctly. Common mistakes: underestimating extras, failing to collect advance payment, and not building a contingency buffer.

  • Break down every cost per person: transport, accommodation (twin sharing), meals, entry fees, activities, tour manager fee, driver tip, emergency fund. Put it in a spreadsheet and share it with the group.
  • Collect at least 50% advance from all members before booking anything. People who haven't paid are the ones who back out last minute — and that changes the per-person cost for everyone else.
  • Build a 10% contingency buffer into the budget for unexpected expenses: a cancelled activity, a medical situation, a hotel upgrade required at the last minute.
  • Identify a single treasurer from the group to manage collections, payments, and expense tracking. Shared responsibility means no responsibility.

Section 3 — Vehicle Selection for Your Group Size

This is one of the most consequential decisions in group travel. The wrong vehicle means discomfort for hours on the road — and mountain roads amplify every minute of that discomfort. Here's a clear guide:

6–8 people Innova Crysta / Ertiga 6 medium bags Family groups, short trips
9–12 people Tempo Traveller (12-seater) 8–10 bags in roof carrier Extended family, friend groups
13–17 people Tempo Traveller (17-seater) 12–14 bags Medium groups, housing society trips

55+ people Multiple coaches Multiple luggage bays Large corporate events, mass pilgrimages

Important: On mountain routes (Manali, Kashmir, Kedarnath approach roads), always verify that your vehicle type is permitted on the specific roads. Some routes restrict large coaches due to sharp switchbacks. Your operator should know this — ask explicitly.

Section 4 — Hotel Bookings for Large Groups

What to confirm before committing to any hotel

  • Negotiate block rates: Most hotels offer 10–20% discounts for block bookings of 10+ rooms. Always ask — don't assume the rate shown online is the group rate.
  • Request rooms on the same floor or adjacent floors. This makes coordinating meal times, departure timings, and emergencies dramatically easier.
  • Confirm early check-in (12 PM or earlier) if your group arrives before 3 PM. Most hotels will accommodate this if rooms are available — but you must negotiate this at booking, not on arrival.
  • Check meal facilities: Can the hotel serve group breakfast (50 pax) at the same time? Some smaller properties can't manage simultaneous service for large groups — staggered breakfast timing may be needed.
  • Get everything in writing: confirmation of room count, meal plan, rates, cancellation policy, and any group-specific arrangements. Verbal agreements in the hospitality industry are not reliable.

Section 5 — Dietary Requirements

Collect requirements 3 weeks before travel

This is where group organisers most commonly trip up. In a group of 25 people, you'll typically have a mix of: pure vegetarians, Jain members (no root vegetables), people with specific allergies, diabetics watching sugar intake, and those with general preferences. Discovering this at the restaurant is chaotic and results in hungry, unhappy travellers.

  • Create a simple Google Form collecting dietary type for every traveller. Share it 3 weeks before departure.
  • Forward the aggregated requirements to your tour operator so hotels and restaurants are briefed in advance.
  • For Jain travellers, confirm specifically with hotels — "Jain food available" at many hotels means "we can skip onion and garlic" but may not mean no root vegetables. Clarify explicitly.
  • Always have a small stock of dry snacks in the vehicle for long drives — someone will always be hungry between meal stops.

Section 6 — Communication and Coordination

How to keep 25 people on the same page

  • Create a dedicated WhatsApp group for the trip — add all travellers plus the tour manager. Use it for timings, meeting points, and updates. Keep it trip-focused; social chatter belongs in a separate group.
  • Share a printed or PDF itinerary with every traveller at least 7 days before departure. Include hotel names, addresses, phone numbers, and day-wise timings. People who don't have this information are a coordination burden on everyone else.
  • Appoint a group coordinator — one reliable person from the group (in addition to the tour manager) who acts as a single point of contact. If someone is late or missing from the bus, this is the person who handles it.
  • Set clear departure times and stick to them. A group that runs 45 minutes late every morning adds up to hours lost over a week-long trip. Brief everyone on Day 1: the bus leaves at the stated time.

Section 7 — Emergency Planning

Things you hope you never need, but must have

  • First aid kit: The tour manager should carry a well-stocked kit including ORS packets (essential in summer), pain relievers, antacids, bandages, antiseptic, and motion sickness tablets — the most commonly needed item on mountain drives.
  • Travel insurance for the group: Most insurance providers offer group travel policies. The per-person cost is minimal; the peace of mind is enormous. If a member requires hospitalisation mid-trip, travel insurance covers it. Without insurance, the group treasurer becomes an improvised hospital billing coordinator.
  • Save local emergency numbers in the tour manager's phone before travel: nearest hospital to each destination, police station, your hotel's number, and the vehicle driver's number.
  • Know who in your group has medical conditions. Collect this information confidentially from all travellers before the trip. The tour manager should know if anyone has diabetes, severe allergies, or a heart condition — not to share it with the group, but to respond appropriately in an emergency.

Section 8 — Why Professional Operators Beat DIY for Large Groups

Here's the honest truth: planning a group tour of 20+ people yourself is possible, but it's a second full-time job for 3–4 months before the trip — and you're still on the hook for everything that goes wrong during it.

A professional operator like Avadhut Tours brings things that money alone can't buy: relationships with hotels in every destination (which means better rates and priority during conflicts), experienced drivers who've driven those specific mountain roads hundreds of times, a tour manager who has handled medical emergencies and hotel complaints and engine breakdowns — and knows exactly what to do. They also carry the liability, which means if something goes wrong, you have someone to call who is contractually obligated to fix it.

The cost difference between a well-managed professional group package and DIY is usually smaller than people expect — and the difference in experience is enormous. Groups that travel with us consistently report that the stress-free experience is worth more than the money saved by trying to coordinate everything themselves.

Quick Checklist — Group Tour of 20+

  • Dates confirmed and approved by all core members
  • Destination selected, permits identified if required
  • Budget calculated per person, contingency included
  • 50% advance collected from all travellers
  • Vehicle type confirmed for group size and route
  • Hotel block booking confirmed in writing
  • Early check-in negotiated where needed
  • Dietary requirements collected from all members
  • Dietary info shared with hotels and restaurants in advance
  • WhatsApp group created, itinerary PDF shared
  • Group coordinator appointed
  • Travel insurance arranged for all members
  • Medical conditions of travellers noted by tour manager
  • Emergency contacts saved: hospital, police, hotels
  • First aid kit packed and assigned to tour manager
  • All travellers have carry documents: ID + hotel list + emergency numbers
