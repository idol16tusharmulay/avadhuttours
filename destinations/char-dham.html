Overview

The Char Dham Yatra

The Char Dham Yatra is one of the holiest pilgrimages in Hinduism — a circuit of four sacred shrines nestled high in the Garhwal Himalayas of Uttarakhand. The four dhams — Yamunotri (source of the Yamuna), Gangotri (source of the Ganges), Kedarnath (one of the 12 Jyotirlingas of Lord Shiva), and Badrinath (dedicated to Lord Vishnu) — together form a journey of immense spiritual significance that millions of devotees undertake every year between May and October.

The yatra is traditionally done in a specific order starting from the west: Yamunotri → Gangotri → Kedarnath → Badrinath. Each shrine sits at altitude between 3,000 m and 3,900 m and requires some trekking (or helicopter access for those with physical limitations). The journey takes 10–14 days by road, passing through breathtaking Himalayan scenery, roaring rivers, and ancient stone temples that have stood for over a thousand years.

Beyond the spiritual dimension, the Char Dham route is one of the most scenic drives in India — the Bhagirathi and Alaknanda river valleys, the cedar and rhododendron forests of Chopta, the high-altitude meadows near Kedarnath, and the Neelkanth peak visible from Badrinath create a landscape of extraordinary power. Important — Medical Fitness & Altitude

All four Char Dham shrines are at high altitude (3,000–3,900 m). Pilgrims with heart conditions, severe asthma, uncontrolled blood pressure or recent surgery should consult their doctor before undertaking this yatra. Altitude sickness (AMS) can affect anyone — acclimatisation days are built into all our itineraries. Helicopter services are available to Kedarnath for those unable to trek.

The Sacred Shrines

The Four Dhams — At a Glance

1
3,291 m · Uttarkashi district

Yamunotri — Goddess Yamuna

Source of the sacred Yamuna River. The temple enshrines a black marble idol of Goddess Yamuna. Reached by a 6 km trek from Janki Chatti — the hot springs (Surya Kund) near the temple are used to cook rice and potatoes for prasad.

2
3,048 m · Uttarkashi district

Gangotri — Goddess Ganga

The origin of the holy Ganges. The Gangotri Temple, built in white granite by a Gorkha commander in the 18th century, sits on the banks of the Bhagirathi River. The actual glacier (Gaumukh) is 19 km further at 4,000 m.

3
3,583 m · Rudraprayag district

Kedarnath — Lord Shiva

One of the 12 Jyotirlingas, the Kedarnath Temple sits in a dramatic setting below the Kedarnath peak. The 16 km trek (or helicopter ride from Phata/Sersi) through flower meadows is the most demanding and most rewarding part of the yatra.

4
3,133 m · Chamoli district

Badrinath — Lord Vishnu

The most accessible of the four dhams (motorable road to the temple). Badrinath Temple, dedicated to Lord Vishnu, sits between Nar and Narayana mountain peaks on the banks of the Alaknanda. Mana Village — the last Indian village before Tibet — is just 3 km away.

Along the Route

Other Highlights on the Char Dham Route

🚁

Kedarnath Helicopter Service

Helicopters operate from Phata, Sersi, Guptkashi and Sitapur to Kedarnath. The 10-minute flight replaces a 16 km trek — ideal for senior pilgrims and those with medical restrictions.

🏘️

Mana Village

India's last inhabited village before the Tibet border, 3 km from Badrinath. Visit the natural cave believed to be where sage Vyasa wrote the Mahabharata, and Bhim Pul — a single rock bridge over the Saraswati River.

🏔️

Neelkanth Peak Views

The snow-clad Neelkanth peak (6,596 m) rises dramatically behind Badrinath and is visible from the temple. Often called the "Queen of Garhwal," it is one of the most photogenic moments of the entire yatra.

🌊

Devprayag Sangam

Where the Bhagirathi and Alaknanda rivers meet to form the Ganges. A sacred bathing ghat with views of the confluence from an ancient temple — a deeply moving spiritual sight en route to Rishikesh.

🌸

Valley of Flowers (add-on)

Near Badrinath, the UNESCO-listed Valley of Flowers blooms with 300+ alpine species from mid-July to mid-August. A stunning extension for those who can manage the additional 3–4 day trek.

🛕

Triyuginarayan Temple

Between Kedarnath and Badrinath lies the Triyuginarayan Temple — believed to be the site of Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati's divine marriage. An eternal flame has reportedly burned here since that day.

Yatra Guidance

Practical Tips for Char Dham Yatra

  1. Register on the Devasthanam Board portal: Online registration at badrinath-kedarnath.gov.in is mandatory before entering the Char Dham shrines. Registration is free — carry the printout and a valid photo ID throughout the yatra.
  2. Book Kedarnath helicopter tickets months ahead: Helicopter services from Phata/Sersi to Kedarnath sell out 2–3 months in advance during peak season (May–June). Book via irctc.co.in (official IRCTC helicopter booking) or authorised operators — avoid middlemen.
  3. Acclimatise — don't rush: Spending a night at Rishikesh or Haridwar before ascending to the dhams helps your body adjust. Never ascend more than 500 m per day above 3,000 m, and rest if you feel headache, nausea or breathlessness (early AMS symptoms).
  4. Carry personal medications and a basic first-aid kit: Stock up on Diamox (altitude sickness medication — consult your doctor first), ORS sachets, basic pain reliever and antiseptic cream. Medical facilities are limited above Rishikesh/Haridwar.
  5. Dress warmly and in layers: Even in May and June, temperatures at Kedarnath (3,583 m) and Badrinath (3,133 m) drop to 0–5°C at night. Carry thermal underlayers, a heavy fleece, a windproof jacket and waterproof trekking shoes. Cotton clothing is inappropriate at altitude.

By Train

Eat like a local

Pure Vegetarian Pahari Fare

Food is half the trip. Here's what to seek out — every Avadhut itinerary leaves room for tasting the local specialties.

  • Aloo ke Gutke — Kumaoni spiced potatoes
  • Bhang ki Chutney — hemp-seed chutney (legal & aromatic)
  • Kafuli — spinach & fenugreek gravy
  • Phaanu — slow-cooked mixed-lentil dal
  • Mandua ki Roti — finger-millet flatbread
  • Bal Mithai — chocolate-fudge sweet from Almora
  • Langar Prasad — free temple food (always vegetarian, often the best meal)
  • Note: meat & alcohol are strictly forbidden in temple towns.
